Part 01 Messaging Audit

Where the current story leaves a gap

Veeva's positioning vs. what I'd put in front of each buyer, specifically

✕ Current framing (observed)

Veeva Commercial Cloud gives you integrated CRM, content, and analytics to drive commercial excellence across your life sciences organization.

✓ Rewrite for the VP of Commercial

Your reps are in front of HCPs with content that's six weeks old and no visibility into whether any of it landed. Veeva Commercial Cloud connects the content you approve to the conversation it ends up in — and tells you what actually moved the prescriber. From approval to impact, in one system.

The shift: Commercial leaders in pharma aren't buying "integrated CRM." They're buying a way to finally understand what's working in the field — because right now they're making million-dollar media decisions based on rep feedback and gut feel. The rewrite names the specific failure mode before it names the product. That's what makes the next sentence land.
✕ Current framing (observed)

Veeva Medical enables medical affairs teams to engage HCPs, manage evidence, and demonstrate medical value with purpose-built cloud solutions.

✓ Rewrite for Medical Affairs

MSLs are fielding off-label questions in the field with no record of what was discussed. That's not just a compliance risk — it's a missed scientific exchange that nobody's learning from. Veeva Medical makes every interaction documentable and every insight searchable, so your medical strategy actually reflects what's happening on the ground.

The shift: Medical Affairs leaders live between two anxieties: regulatory exposure and scientific irrelevance. The current copy addresses neither directly. The rewrite leads with the compliance moment (undocumented off-label discussion) and then pivots to value — the strategic intelligence medical teams are leaving on the table every day. Name the moment, then name the fix.
✕ Current framing (observed)

Veeva's true multitenant cloud platform delivers industry-specific solutions built for life sciences, with continuous innovation and lower total cost of ownership.

✓ Rewrite for the CIO

Your last enterprise software deployment took 18 months and three consulting firms. Veeva configures in weeks, not quarters, because it's built for one industry and one industry only. No customization debt. No compatibility fires every time the business changes a process. Just a system that keeps up with the product pipeline it's supposed to support.

The shift: IT leaders in pharma have scar tissue from Salesforce customizations and SAP rollouts that never quite worked. "True multitenant cloud" is a technical spec that means nothing until you tie it to a memory they have. "18 months and three consulting firms" is that memory. Get there first, and the product story writes itself.
Part 02 GTM Brief — Sample Deliverable

Launching Veeva Vault MedComms in a new geography

How I'd think through taking a mature product into a market segment it hasn't fully penetrated

Sample deliverable

The opportunity

Vault MedComms is already in use at top-20 pharma globally. But mid-size European biotech — especially those in Germany and the Nordics building out medical affairs functions for the first time — represent a large, mostly untouched segment. They have the compliance need. They don't yet have a process, which means they're not looking for a replacement. They're looking for a starting point.

That's a very different conversation to have. And a much easier one to win.

Why mid-size biotech, why now

European biotech IPOs and Series B/C rounds hit a multi-year high in 2023–24. A significant cohort of companies have recently crossed the threshold where they need a proper medical affairs infrastructure — but haven't yet committed to a platform.

The window is the year before they build it wrong themselves. Getting into the evaluation set now is the only way to be there when the budget unlocks.

The four triggers that open a conversation

🧬
First regulatory submission approaching
Companies within 12–18 months of their first EMA or MHRA submission are suddenly very aware of what they can and can't document. That's the moment to show up with Vault.
👥
First MSL hire
Hiring your first Medical Science Liaison is a signal that medical affairs is becoming real. At this point, the process question is wide open. Veeva MedComms should be part of how they answer it.
📋
Post-trial evidence generation phase
Phase III data readout creates a surge in medical content — publications, field decks, congress abstracts. If there's no system to manage it, it's chaos. That's a very specific pain we can name.
🔀
Partnership or licensing deal signed
When a larger pharma licenses a biotech's asset, the biotech suddenly has to operate at the commercial partner's standards. Vault becomes the obvious infrastructure for that upgrade.

The message that actually opens doors at this company size

"You don't have a legacy system to replace. You have a blank sheet of paper, and that's actually an advantage — if you use it to build something that scales with your pipeline instead of constraining it."

Competitors lead with features. For a company building their medical affairs function from scratch, the right lead is permission to start correctly. Vault isn't the complicated enterprise software they're afraid of. It's the thing that means they never have to rip out a homegrown spreadsheet system in three years.

How the launch runs

Weeks 1–3
Build the target list. Pull biotech companies in Germany, Nordics, and Benelux that: (a) have a late-stage pipeline asset, (b) have medical affairs headcount under 15, and (c) aren't already using a commercial platform. This is a discrete, findable universe. Use clinical trial registries, LinkedIn headcount signals, and press releases from trial readouts. Start with 100–150 accounts.
Weeks 4–5
Build the "blank sheet" content package. One whitepaper: "What a best-in-class medical affairs infrastructure looks like for a company preparing for its first launch." No product pitch. Pure education. This becomes the SDR's opening value exchange — not a cold demo request, a useful thing they can share with the medical affairs lead.
Weeks 6–8
Activate through clinical networks, not just outbound. Sponsor or partner with a medical affairs community event (Medical Affairs Professional Society Europe is the obvious one). The audience there is exactly this profile. Put Veeva subject matter experts in front of them, not sales people. The credibility is in the clinical depth, not the pitch.
Week 8+
Measure what matters. Not just pipeline generated. Track content downloads from target accounts, event conversation quality from the MSA activation, and time-to-second-meeting. The first metric tells us if the message is landing. The second tells us if we're getting the right people in the room. The third tells us if we're wasting sales team time on misqualified accounts.

How we know it's working

Primary
ICP→Pipeline
% of target-list accounts that progress to a qualified discovery conversation within 60 days
Secondary
Content Reach
Whitepaper downloads from target accounts (not total downloads — that's the wrong number)
Leading signal
Event Quality
Number of follow-up conversations booked from the MAPS Europe activation vs. prior event benchmarks

Part 04 Copy Samples

Three different moments in the buying cycle, three different people, three different levels of familiarity with what Veeva actually does. None of these are written for a persona document. They're written for the specific moment of anxiety the buyer is in when they encounter them.

Thought leadership article — for VP of Medical Affairs at mid-size biotech Thought Leadership
Your MSLs are having the most important scientific conversations in your company. None of them are on record.
Ask any VP of Medical Affairs how their MSL team is performing and you'll get a version of the same answer: "They're having great conversations. HCPs love them. We're seeing good engagement."

Then ask what was discussed in those conversations, which data drove the most follow-up questions, and what's showing up repeatedly across geographies — and the answer changes. Usually to: "We get a call summary. Sometimes."

This isn't a people problem. It's a systems problem. MSLs are doing exactly what they're supposed to do: building scientific relationships in real time, often without wifi, in between back-to-back HCP meetings. Asking them to be the documentation system on top of that is where the institutional knowledge gets lost.

The companies that figure this out first aren't doing anything dramatic. They're just capturing what's already happening — systematically, compliantly, in a way that feeds back into the medical strategy instead of disappearing into a shared folder somewhere.
Why it works: Opens with a recognition moment — the VP will nod along to the first three sentences because they've had that exact conversation. Then lands the reframe: this isn't a performance issue, it's an infrastructure issue. That moves the solution from "fix your people" to "give them the right system," which is the conversation Veeva wants to be having.
Sales enablement talk track — for SDR opening on IT/CIO cold outreach Sales Enablement
"What would it cost your team if we told you that the commercial platform you're running right now isn't going to be fit for purpose in 18 months?"
That's not the opener. That's where you want to get in the first five minutes. Here's how to get there:

The actual opener: "I know your team is usually stuck cleaning up what the business decided on a system, rather than being part of the decision. I wanted to have a different kind of conversation — what does your current life sciences tech stack actually cost you to maintain, not in licensing, but in IT hours?"

Let them answer. They will. CIOs in pharma have a number in their head and nobody ever asks for it.

From there, every door is open:
→ "Our customization debt is significant" → Veeva's no-customization architecture
→ "Upgrades are a nightmare" → continuous delivery without upgrade projects
→ "We have seven systems doing what should be three" → Vault consolidation story
→ "The business keeps changing the requirements" → configurability without code

The goal of this call isn't a demo. It's getting the CIO to say "I've never thought about it that way" out loud. That's the moment they become a champion instead of a blocker.
Why it works: CIOs are almost always positioned as the budget gatekeeper in enterprise SaaS sales. This reframes them as a strategic partner with their own pain — which they have, and nobody's acknowledging. The question about IT hours rather than licensing cost catches them off guard in a useful way. It shows you understand their world, not just the product you're selling.
Customer story framework — turning a win into a replicable narrative Customer Marketing
The customer story that actually moves the next deal isn't about features. It's about the moment the team realized the old way wasn't working anymore.
Most life sciences case studies follow the same arc: company had a problem, company implemented Veeva, numbers improved. By paragraph two, the reader already knows where it's going.

The stories that actually get referenced in sales conversations — the ones that get forwarded between peers before a buying decision — start somewhere different. They start with a specific, recognizable failure moment.

A good Veeva customer story framework:

01 / The moment: What specific event made the old way undeniable? (A regulatory audit that revealed undocumented MSL exchanges. A product launch where nobody could pull together which HCPs had been reached with which message. A compliance hold that delayed a campaign by six weeks.)

02 / The decision: What made them choose to act now rather than later, and why Veeva? (Not "the platform's industry specificity" — that's marketing language. The real reason, in their words.)

03 / The change: What's different in the way the team actually works? Not just the numbers — the day-to-day experience of doing the job.

04 / What they'd tell a peer: This is the quote that gets forwarded. "Before Veeva, I was explaining to the board why I couldn't tell them which markets were performing. Now I can show them in 20 minutes." That's the story.
Why it works: The JD specifically calls out proactively finding customer success stories and making them usable for the field. This framework is what I'd use to brief the customer interviews. It's designed to extract the moment and the quote, not just the metrics — because moments and quotes are what live in the back of a buyer's mind when they're deciding.
